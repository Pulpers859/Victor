# Liabilities And Protection Playbook

This playbook covers how Victor should interpret `Debts`, `Insurance`, and liability-sensitive account or contract context.

## `Debts` Sheet

### What Victor Is Looking For

- whether debt is a true emergency, a manageable drag, or a federal-loan strategy question
- whether the household is confusing low-rate debt with harmless debt
- whether federal-loan decisions are being oversimplified

### Debt Hierarchy

Victor should sort debts into buckets:

1. high-interest consumer debt
2. federal student loans
3. private student loans
4. vehicle or other amortizing debt
5. mortgage debt

Each bucket deserves different treatment.

### Interpretation Rules

- `Federal loan? = Yes`
  This is a stop sign against casual refinance advice.
- `PSLF eligible?`
  This should trigger a deeper modeling mindset, not a fast answer.
- `Qualifying payments`
  These matter because sunk time in PSLF can materially change the optimal strategy.
- `Interest rate`
  High rates matter, but rate alone is not enough for federal loans because embedded federal options have value.
- `Status / notes`
  This field is often where the real risk hides: deferment, forbearance history, servicer confusion, or uncertain eligibility.

### Things Victor Must Ask Before Any Major Federal-Loan Recommendation

- exact federal loan type
- current repayment plan
- servicer
- PSLF status and qualifying count
- employer type
- filing status
- spouse income and loans when relevant
- likely future income change

### What Victor Should Not Say Casually

- “Just refinance.”
- “Low-interest debt is always good debt.”
- “You should always invest instead of paying debt.”

Those are slogans, not physician-finance analysis.

## `Insurance` Sheet

### What Victor Is Looking For

- whether future earning power is protected
- whether the client is relying on employer coverage alone
- whether dependents create a real life-insurance need
- whether legal and job-transition risks are being ignored

### Interpretation Rules

- Missing own-occupation disability coverage is a real physician red flag, especially during training or early attending years.
- Employer-only disability coverage may be better than nothing, but it should not automatically be treated as sufficient.
- Term life is usually a tool for dependency and obligation protection, not wealth building.
- Contract-review omissions can be wealth leaks even when the investment plan looks clean.

### Protection Order

1. prevent catastrophe
2. preserve earning power
3. reduce avoidable liability exposure
4. optimize taxes and investments afterward

## Cross-Sheet Warning Signs

- large student-loan balance plus no disability coverage
- attending income plus minimal liquidity
- strong retirement balances plus weak contract protection
- house plans plus unstable income or poor emergency reserves
- spouse/dependents plus no clear life-insurance rationale
