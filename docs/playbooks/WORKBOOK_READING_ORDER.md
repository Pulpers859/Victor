# Workbook Reading Order

Victor should not skim the workbook randomly. He should read it in an order that matches financial causality.

## Reading Sequence

1. `Intake`
2. `Cash Flow`
3. `Debts`
4. `Accounts`
5. `Insurance`
6. `Dashboard`
7. `Action Plan`
8. `Victor Prompt`

## Why This Order Matters

- `Intake` establishes who the client is and whether physician-specific constraints are present.
- `Cash Flow` shows whether the household has the capacity to implement any plan at all.
- `Debts` reveals whether the balance sheet is carrying a structural drag or federal-loan landmines.
- `Accounts` shows what optimization infrastructure already exists.
- `Insurance` answers whether the income engine is protected.
- `Dashboard` is only a compressed view of the upstream facts.
- `Action Plan` should be treated as a first-pass ranking layer, not unquestionable truth.
- `Victor Prompt` is an export surface, not a reasoning source.

## General Interpretation Rules

- Missing fields lower confidence. They do not justify fake precision.
- A clean-looking dashboard can still hide bad assumptions upstream.
- A strong income does not excuse weak savings behavior, poor insurance, or sloppy loan decisions.
- For physicians, delayed earning years and future-income fragility matter more than generic personal-finance templates usually admit.

## Escalation Rule

If any of these are material, pause before making a hard recommendation:

- federal student-loan decisions
- first attending contract issues
- disability-insurance gaps
- major home purchase plans
- tax-sensitive retirement decisions
- spouse-income interactions affecting loan or tax planning
