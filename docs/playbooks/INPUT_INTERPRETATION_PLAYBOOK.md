# Input Interpretation Playbook

This playbook covers how Victor should interpret `Intake`, `Cash Flow`, and the non-liability parts of `Accounts`.

## `Intake` Sheet

### What Victor Is Looking For

- physician stage: resident, fellow, attending, or private practice
- whether this is a temporary low-income phase or a stable attending phase
- household structure that changes risk and liquidity needs
- whether the stated goals are realistic relative to the actual cash-flow base

### What The Fields Mean

- `Training stage`
  This is one of the most important fields in the workbook. It changes how Victor should interpret loans, savings rates, housing decisions, and insurance priorities.
- `Expected attending income`
  This is useful for planning transitions, but it must not be treated as current spendable money.
- `Gross household income`
  This is the denominator for savings-rate judgment, but gross alone can mislead if taxes, spouse context, or retirement deductions are missing.
- `Monthly take-home pay`
  This is often more operationally useful than gross income for day-to-day planning.
- `Income stability`
  This should directly influence liquidity targets and risk capacity.
- `Near-term goals`
  Victor should test whether these goals fit the cash-flow reality instead of merely restating them.

### Questions Victor Should Ask If Intake Is Weak

- Is the physician still in training or already an attending?
- Is the income expected to rise soon, or is that assumption vague?
- Is there spouse income that materially changes household flexibility?
- Is a house purchase goal timing-driven, status-driven, or financially justified?

## `Cash Flow` Sheet

### What Victor Is Looking For

- whether the household has a surplus at all
- whether savings rate is a behavior problem or simply an income-phase problem
- whether liquidity is structurally weak
- whether “investing problems” are actually spending problems

### How To Read The Core Fields

- `Monthly fixed expenses`
  These show how trapped the household is before discretionary spending even begins.
- `Monthly variable expenses`
  This is where lifestyle inflation and unexamined leakage often hide.
- `Monthly debt payments`
  This measures how much of the monthly plan is already pre-sold to past decisions.
- `Monthly savings and investing`
  This is the base engine for long-term wealth building.
- `Cash buffer`
  This is not just a comfort measure. It determines whether the household can absorb shocks without sabotaging the long-term plan.
- `Target emergency-fund months`
  This should reflect income stability, dependents, job risk, and upcoming obligations rather than internet defaults.

### Interpretation Rules

- A weak surplus means portfolio optimization is not the primary bottleneck.
- A large gap between gross income and low savings often signals lifestyle inflation or under-tracked spending.
- High income with low liquidity is not strength. It is fragility wearing a white coat.
- Large planned bonuses should not be treated as a stable base unless the income really is predictable and recurring.

## `Accounts` Sheet

### What Victor Is Looking For

- whether tax-advantaged space exists but is underused
- whether an employer match is being wasted
- whether the client already has a reasonable retirement structure
- whether balances are concentrated in the wrong places

### Interpretation Rules

- Employer match outranks clever portfolio positioning.
- Retirement-account underuse is a real issue only after liquidity and high-interest debt are controlled.
- Account balances alone do not prove discipline. Contribution behavior matters more.
- If annual contribution limits are listed, Victor must still verify current-year official limits before making a live recommendation that depends on them.

## What Victor Should Not Infer Too Fast

- A strong career trajectory does not prove strong financial discipline.
- A low current savings rate during residency is not automatically failure.
- A high current savings rate is not automatically impressive if the household is uninsured, overleveraged, or ignoring federal-loan value.
